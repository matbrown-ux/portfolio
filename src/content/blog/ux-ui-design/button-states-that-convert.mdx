## Why button states matter

Most buttons have five states: default, hover, focus, active, and disabled. Most implementations only style two of them.

This is a missed opportunity, not just for accessibility, but for the kind of micro-polish that separates professional interfaces from amateur ones.

## The five states

**Default**: the button at rest. Clean, readable, with enough visual weight to signal "this is clickable."

**Hover**: the button responding to cursor proximity. Use a subtle shift: color change, slight lift with `translateY(-2px)`, or a shadow. Not all three at once.

**Focus**: critical for keyboard navigation and accessibility. Use a visible `outline` or `ring` that passes WCAG 4.5:1 contrast against the background. Never `outline: none` without a replacement.

**Active**: the button being pressed. A slight compress (`scale(0.98)`) communicates "I heard you" to the user.

**Disabled**: the button that can't be clicked. Reduce opacity to 40–50%, change the cursor to `not-allowed`, and remove all hover effects.

## Implementation

```tsx
<button
  className="px-6 py-3 bg-vermilion text-cream
    hover:-translate-y-0.5 hover:bg-vermilion-hover
    focus:outline-none focus:ring-2 focus:ring-vermilion focus:ring-offset-2 focus:ring-offset-directors-black
    active:scale-[0.98]
    disabled:opacity-40 disabled:cursor-not-allowed disabled:translate-y-0
    transition-all duration-200"
>
  Click me
</button>
```

The key insight: each state communicates something specific. Design them as a system, not as afterthoughts.
